# Animated Shorts

Use this reference when planning, writing, reviewing, or producing a short animated film, cartoon scene, storyboard, or animatic.

## Start With The Smallest Story

A short animation works best when it has a clear change:

- Character: who or what the audience follows.
- Want: what the character is trying to do.
- Obstacle: what gets in the way.
- Turn: what changes the situation.
- Final beat: the last clear emotional or comic note.

If the story cannot be described in one sentence, simplify before adding shots.

## Beat Sheet

For a short piece, use a compact beat sheet:

| Beat | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Setup | Establish character, place, and expectation. |
| Trigger | Something changes or interrupts the normal state. |
| Attempt | The character acts. |
| Complication | The action does not go as expected. |
| Turn | The character or situation changes direction. |
| Button | The final image, joke, emotion, or reveal. |

Not every short needs dialogue. Many animated shorts are stronger when action and staging carry the story.

## Shot Planning

Plan shots before polishing animation:

- Wide shot: establishes place, scale, or relationship.
- Medium shot: shows action clearly.
- Close-up: shows decision, emotion, object detail, or punchline.
- Insert: isolates an important prop or clue.
- Moving shot: follows action or reveals space.

Use one main idea per shot. If a shot needs many explanations, split it.

## Storyboard Notes

Each storyboard panel should answer:

- What changed from the previous panel?
- Where should the viewer look first?
- What is the character doing or deciding?
- Is the silhouette readable?
- What is the camera distance?
- How long does the moment need?

Use simple drawings, text panels, or thumbnails. Clarity matters more than polish.

## Animatic Timing

An animatic tests timing before final animation.

- Hold longer on decisions, reveals, and emotional turns.
- Cut faster through obvious actions.
- Leave room for anticipation before important motion.
- Let the final beat breathe.
- Watch the animatic without reading notes; the story should still track.

If the animatic is unclear, the final animation will usually stay unclear.

## Character Action

Character motion should reveal intention:

- Anticipation shows that the character is about to act.
- Pose clarity shows what the character wants.
- Follow-through shows weight, energy, or personality.
- Stillness can show thought, shock, fear, confidence, or timing.
- Eye direction and head turns can guide audience attention.

The body should not move randomly. Movement should come from want, reaction, or physical cause.

## Acting Without Dialogue

For silent or mostly visual animation:

- Use clear poses.
- Use props as story anchors.
- Use contrast between expectation and result.
- Use timing to make the audience anticipate.
- Let the character react after important events.

The audience should understand the situation even with the sound off.

## Scene Review

Review animated scenes in this order:

1. Story clarity: can the viewer explain what happened?
2. Staging: is the main action readable?
3. Timing: are holds, cuts, and actions paced well?
4. Acting: does the movement show intention?
5. Continuity: do screen direction, props, and character position track?
6. Polish: secondary motion, effects, lighting, and texture.

Do not polish secondary animation before story, staging, and timing work.

## Output Formats

Depending on the task, produce:

- One-sentence premise.
- Beat sheet.
- Shot list with duration estimates.
- Storyboard panel descriptions.
- Animatic timing notes.
- Character action notes.
- Scene review findings.
- Production checklist.
